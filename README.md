Hotel Booking Dashboard

This is one of my first data analytics projects and honestly it taught me more than I expected. What looks like a simple dashboard on the outside took a lot of work to get right.

What this project is about

I worked with a real hotel bookings dataset containing 87,397 bookings from 2015 to 2017 covering two hotels, City Hotel and Resort Hotel. Starting from raw messy data, I cleaned it, analysed it and built a full interactive dashboard in Excel.

Tool I used

Microsoft Excel including pivot tables, charts, slicers, conditional formatting and insight strips.

What I actually did

I started with raw data that needed cleaning. I removed duplicates, fixed column names, corrected data types and created a few new calculated columns like Total Guests which combines adults, children and babies.

Then I built pivot analysis across the dataset to understand patterns before touching any charts.

The final dashboard has 6 KPI cards, 5 charts, 3 slicers that filter everything at once, annotations pointing to key moments in the data, and insight strips under each chart explaining what the numbers mean in plain English.

What I found in the data

The most surprising finding was that Non-Refund bookings cancel almost 95% of the time. That means guests are booking just to hold a date and then cancelling anyway. The deposit policy is clearly not working as intended.

Online Travel Agents bring in the highest average daily rate at 118 dollars per night, closely followed by Direct bookings at 117 dollars. Corporate guests pay significantly less at 68 dollars which raised questions about whether those contracts are being negotiated well.

Portugal accounts for 31 percent of all guests. UK and France are the top international markets.

Contract guests stay an average of 6 nights which is double the stay length of Transient guests. This suggests contract bookings are more valuable for occupancy stability.

City Hotel grew steadily every year and peaked at 3,070 bookings in a single month in May 2017. Resort Hotel stayed relatively flat throughout the same period.

What I learnt

Dashboards look simple from the outside. Going through the full process from raw data to a finished dashboard taught me that every step, cleaning, analysis, chart design, layout, annotations, is its own skill. Nothing is as quick as it looks when someone else has already done it.

I also learnt that the most important part of a dashboard is not the charts themselves but the story you tell around them. Numbers without context are just numbers.
